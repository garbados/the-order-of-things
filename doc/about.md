# About "The Order of Things"

Today, we call Uruk the first city,
or just a ruin in a salted waste.
In its time, nomads called it insane.
Some permanent settlements counted populations in the hundreds,
but Uruk and its cult of the clever and ambitious Inanna
mounted such madness so to host thousands with the wealth
of the stores of Eanna and the tamed Tigris and Euphrates.
Those rivers wander, westerner. They are not cooperative neighbors,
but as temperamental and tricksy as Coyote and Crow.
It was religious fervor that civilized them,
a civilization of honey bread and cleansing oils
and such articulate sensuality.

By the time of Gilgamesh, Uruk had already grown great.
The power of the cult had yielded to the administrator,
the En, who you misunderstand as *king*,
whose tyranny could only be matched by the wild green.
A high priestess went out to tame Enkidu, the wild man,
the mirror of her unruly king,
and asked a question that would echo for millennia:
"Would you like me to teach you?"
The rest, they say, is history,
or perhaps a mythology.

By the time Akkad translated the Epic of Gilgamesh
into one of the ancestors of Arabic,
Uruk's innovations had become commonplace.
What we call cities had become the bones of the bodies of empires,
while Uruk's power waned with age.
Its agricultural innovations proved insufficient for the trials of eons;
slowly but surely, its wards seeded the earth with salt,
until nothing could grow, and nothing could live,
and then there was nothing to leave behind
but the bones of the bodies that first dreamed of empire.
Eanna stands roofless, open to the sky from whence it came.
Even Akkad was swallowed, into the sprawl of great Baghdad.
Ambitions may outlive individuals,
but change is eternity's only lasting companion.

By the time of Akkad, Inanna had syncretized into Ishtar,
her distinguishing divinity reduced to that delicious sensuality,
as city-scale cuisine and hygiene had become widespread norms,
so that when the westerners of modernity began to translate it,
they would translate the high priestess of Inanna teaching Enkidu
as a harlot being sent to be used.
What have the barbarians forgotten?
How has progress uncivilized them?
What does it mean that they do not season their food,
or wash their hands or feet or bottoms?
Even the tyranny of a covetous En was never so savage as this.

"The Order of Things" is not about Uruk
or Gilgamesh or Enkidu or Inanna,
but only the themes their myths navigate.
It is a reflection, seven thousand years later,
not a retelling.
It is about civilization, and guestright, and love.
It is about death, and return, and enlightenment.
It is a love letter to a place whose echoes
still shake and shape the world.
